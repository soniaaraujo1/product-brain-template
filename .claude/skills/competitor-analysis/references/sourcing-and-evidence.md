# Sourcing and evidence

Competitive intel is only as good as its sources. The website is the *least* reliable channel (it's their marketing). Go wide, tag everything, and separate shipped from announced.

## Multi-channel source checklist (don't stop at the website)

Work down this list for a full teardown; pick the relevant few for lighter modes.

- **Vendor site + newsroom/press** — product pages, docs/help center, release notes, case studies, pricing page. Baseline, but treat as claims.
- **LinkedIn** — company page + founder/exec posts. **Announcements often land here (or on stage) before, or instead of, the website.** Search `site:linkedin.com <vendor> <feature>`; read the actual posts.
- **Glassdoor** — employee reviews: company health, growth, sales culture, and roadmap-clarity gripes (internal signal you can't get elsewhere). Check for a "layoffs" review filter.
- **Review sites** — G2, TrustRadius, Gartner Peer Insights, SelectHub, Capterra. Real user pros/cons, feature mentions (evidence a feature is used, not just shipped), ratings, and often **pricing**. Note: G2/TrustRadius/Gartner frequently **block direct fetch** — fall back to search snippets and say so.
- **Analysts** — whoever covers your category (e.g. Gartner Market Guide / Peer Insights, Forrester, and the specialist analysts for your space). Best for market-structure and "who really leads."
- **Community** — the subreddits, Slack/Discord groups, and forums where your buyers and users hang out. Unfiltered voice. A *thin* community footprint is itself a finding (enterprise-sales-led, not bottoms-up).
- **Company/financial data** — Crunchbase, Tracxn, GetLatka, PitchBook/CB Insights. Funding, revenue/headcount estimates (label as estimates).
- **Video / webinar / demos** — YouTube, webinar recordings, conference talks. The best evidence of what a product *actually does* vs. claims.
- **Pricing signals** — SelectHub, Vendr, review-site mentions, RFP notes. Often the only way past "contact sales."
- **Our own field** — sales win/loss, AE/CSM anecdotes, customer feedback in `Customer-Feedback/`. Primary and high-signal.

## Evidence tiers (tag every claim)

- **A — Verified fact.** Primary or independent source, ideally with a date: docs/demo/help-center, review naming the feature in use, case study, analyst report, filing. Cite the URL + date.
- **B — Vendor claim.** Marketing copy, press release, sales deck. True-ish but self-interested; label it.
- **C — Inference / analysis.** Our reasoning. Clearly mark as ours, not fact.

A capability scores well only on tier-A evidence. Recommendations should not rest on tier-C alone.

## Real vs. hype test

For any "it's live / it's agentic / it does X" claim, ask: **shipped or announced?**
- **Shipped** = product-level evidence (docs, a demo video, a review or case study naming it in use).
- **Announced** = PR, LinkedIn, or stage only, with no product trace.
Announced-and-analyst-endorsed is still not shipped. Classify each headline capability real / hype / unclear.

## Recency and consistency

- **Date-stamp** findings; prioritize the last 12-18 months; flag anything stale.
- **Cross-check the vendor's own numbers.** If data-scale/revenue/customer figures are inconsistent across their sources (common), report the range and call the inconsistency out — it undercuts an "authoritative" posture and is a legitimate talking point.
- Note **blocked or thin** channels explicitly, so the reader sees the edges of the research rather than assuming full coverage.
